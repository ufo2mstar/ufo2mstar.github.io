A cousin recently called me out in the family WhatsApp group for being a ghost. Fair enough. I am one. Here's what I sent back:

> Haha.. Happy Easter na!
> Where do I even start!
> Truth be told, I take cover under the pretext that I have a strict group mute policy, but honestly it is just that it is very difficult for me to get back to anyone on the group in time (I mean before I have a chance to realize what's goin on, you guys most likely have moved on to the next topic).. Apart from me being terrible at communication, with the infrequency of my checking my phone (let alone WhatsApp) it's kinda tough for me to reply on time to anything at all..
> Thus my message consumption, esp. in the groups, has become very staggered and asynchronous..
> But I do love to stay connected, at least as an end point node in the network, (so keep em coming!), and I do love the updates I occasionally actually do catch (such as your daughter1's atangatram ;) looking forward for daughter2's too, soon!)
> And besides, I'm in some ten or so groups and even with just an average day of some 30 odd updates in each of them, my WhatsApp feed blows up pretty quickly!
> Honestly, I wish I was a better juggler! Perhaps that's the reason I don't have time for Facebook either or any social media for that matter..
> It has all roughly blurred into my news cycle and I treat all the messages with the same reverence and reciprocation that I'm sure we all do to a newspaper article!
> ie: My love and well wishes for you guys (as you matter a lot to me!) aside, I treat our groups with Awwww or awe or a chuckle every now and again!

That message took me probably fifteen minutes to write. For a WhatsApp reply. On a holiday. To family.

And that's kind of the whole problem.

## I Am Not a Machine Gun Texter

Some people text the way they breathe - short bursts, one word at a time, fired off without a second thought. A thought arrives, a thumb moves, a message lands. The conversation is a stream and they're swimming in it.

I am not that person. I am the person who reads the stream, thinks about it, drafts a response in my head, reconsiders, opens the app, types half of it, deletes it, retypes it with better phrasing, and then sends it forty minutes after everyone has moved on to something else entirely.

My wife has pointed out - more than once - that my casual text messages are "way too high quality." She means it as a gentle roast. She's not wrong. I will wordsmith a grocery list reminder if I'm not careful. It's one of the perils of perfectionism applied indiscriminately - the inability to match the effort to the stakes.

## The Asynchronous Node

I described myself in that message as "an end point node in the network." The CS brain leaking into personal communication, sure, but it's also the most accurate description I have. I'm connected. I care about the data flowing through. I'm just not a router - I don't forward, amplify, or respond in real time. I'm more like a log collector. Everything arrives eventually, gets processed in batch, and most of it produces no visible output, but is stored in the magnetic tapes of my mind - available for retrieval at the time of need.

The newspaper metaphor I used was honest too, even if it sounds cold. Ten groups, thirty messages each, that's three hundred messages a day competing for the same attention I'm trying to give to work, to whatever book I'm reading, to the actual people physically near me. At some point it all becomes a feed, and feeds get skimmed. I read. I react internally. I move on. The people in those groups probably think I don't care. I do. I'm just terrible at showing it at the speed the medium demands.

## The Tension

I'm not going to pretend this is purely a virtue. "I think deeply before I respond" sounds noble until you realize it means your cousin thinks you don't give a damn about her daughter's arangetram. The perfectionism that makes me redraft a WhatsApp message three times is the same perfectionism that makes me not send one at all when I can't get it right. A quick "congrats!!" with two exclamation marks would have been better than my silence followed by an eloquent apology three weeks later. I know this.

But I also know I'm not going to change it. Not fundamentally. I've tried the "just fire off a quick reply" approach and it feels wrong in my hands - like writing with my non-dominant hand. The compromise I've landed on - and it's imperfect - is to be honest about it. This message was my way of saying: I'm here, I'm reading, I love you all, and my silence is not indifference. It's just how I'm wired. Take it or leave it, but at least now you know.
